US

US stocks spent the week largely in the doldrums, retreating for a third week in the last four ahead of the Federal Reserve's key monetary policy meeting in the middle of next week. Investors took a cautious approach to the possibility that the central bank will start to taper its quantitative easing policy, even as a June measure of consumer sentiment declined and the International Monetary Fund cut its US growth forecast for next year. The Dow Jones dropped 1.17% over the week to end at 15,070.18, the S&P 500 fell 1.01% to close at 1,626.73, while the Nasdaq declined 1.32% to settle at 3,423.56.

JPMorgan Chase said Friday that it is spinning off its private equity arm, One Equity Partners, as part of efforts to streamline its operations and attract outside funding. The bank said the fund's next capital raising round would be sourced exclusively from external limited partners. The move is expected to smooth the bank's earnings and avoid any potential non-compliance by the bank with the Volcker rule.

The Gannett Company on Thursday continued to diversify away from its ailing print publishing business via the purchase for $1.5 billion of the Belo Corporation, nearly doubling its ownership of local television stations to 43 from 23 in one move. Gannett stock surged 34% on the announcement of the deal, which will raise to two-thirds the proportion of earnings sourced from digital and broadcasting operations.

India's Apollo Tyres on Thursday swooped in to purchase Cooper Tire and Rubber for $2.5 billion, making the acquisition the largest by an Indian firm of a US counterpart to date. The deal, the latest in an increasingly busy automotive sector, will allow Apollo to spread risk away from the slowdown in its core India market, particularly as Cooper has a strong European presence in addition to being the fourth largest US tire manufacturer by sales.

Cosmetics firm Coty on Wednesday priced its initial public offering at $17.50 per share, in the middle of its expected range. The offering garnered just under $1 billion in proceeds in one of the largest IPOs in the US this year, which values the firm at about $6.7 billion. However, shares debuted into a shaky market Thursday, holding flat at their offer price before closing down 1.14% at $17.30.

US tech giants including Google, Microsoft, Facebook and Yahoo! began the week on the defensive after a series of revelations linking them to a US government surveillance scheme known as PRISM, which allegedly allows National Security Agency officials to extract data on users directly from their servers. Apple and Facebook executives led a public relations drive to vindicate their firms, arguing the program was very limited. The New York Times and others media outlets reported that the firms had discussed options for allowing the government access to certain data, though questions remain over the hurdles that had to be negotiated before it could be accessed.
